About

My name is Nathan Deinlein. Currently, I am working as the Manager of Data Engineering for Frontier Airlines. I have spent the last two and a half years at Frontier, working to hone my skills in both data engineering, visualization, and data integration. Prior to this, I was working on a Data and Process analyst for a Tangoe, handling onboarding and solution architecting of data for new customers and existing customers utilizing the logistics services Tangoe provided.

In 2022, I completed my masters degree in Data Science from Southern Methodist University. I am constantly looking to continue my growth as a professional in the Data Science space. This website is a chance for me to display some of my work as continue that journey.

Capstone Project

While still in school, I changed jobs to allow me to work more in the field that I was attempting to enter. I joined Frontier Airlines in their revenue management team as a data analyst. At Frontier, I have worked in a number of capacities allowing me to dabble in many areas of data science. My first tasks on arriving were to extract information from CRM portals and transform that data using excel to create visualizations. As this was extremely manual, I proceeded to map our main databases, and create a logic dictionary to build reporting on. I then automated the processes, first through use of python scripts and a VM, but then through pyspark and Databricks when that tool became available to us. I then set about building medallion architecture tables for most of the revenue management reporting needs within my department. Building on this framework, we started to create customer centric reporting and personalized marking campaigns using machine learning applications and pipelines. Once these were deployed we looked at monitoring the results to ensure statistical significance between the control and test groups of our marketing campaigns. From there I was asked to create a child seating algorithm to automatically place children 13 and younger with an adult from their booking for free. This was in order to meet a request from the DOT, and I completed the work in three weeks. Building the first automated algorithm in the country. Similar algorithms have been released since by Alaska and JetBlue. But major airlines like Delta, United Airlines, and American Airlines have not built anything similar to my knowledge.

In September of this year, I took over as Manager of Data Engineering. Currently, I manage a team of 26 data engineers to create pipelines for everything from basic data replication, to streaming of social media data responses. I am leading projects like retirement of old systems (Microsoft Synapse & SQL), our movement to cloud platforms (Databricks and Snowflake), and creation of our Customer 360 platform that utilizes customer aggregated data through Databricks, as well as connecting with loyalty information, DOT complains, and real time event tracking for customer displacement through Microsoft Dynamics 365.

Back

This was the final assignment for our database management class. I worked with a group of two other students to conduct research into the ingestion of data from an API as well as its final state in a SQL versus noSQL database. My section was to go through parsing out the data retrieved through the API into set columns within an SQL database. Though we could define tags within the data coming across, it's unstructured nature was not entirely conducive to an SQL environment. Instead, due to rate of ingestion, and then usability of the data after ingestion, we suggested the use of a noSQL database, in this case MongoDB, for our purposes, should be tasked with a similar project in the future. Since completing this project, I have been working much more in Azure Datalakes and Databricks in particular with ingestion of files at a rapid pase that can then be parsed later to flatten them if needed.

Machine Learning was a fascination I had going into my masters. For our first class on it, we were split into groups and were given a dataset to try various algorithms on to test our understanding. Our data happened to be on horse racing. We set out with two main goals, the first to be able to predict a winner of any given race, and then the second goal was to predict the top 3, or placers, for any given race. I have below here our final notebook for the project that utilized a number of concepts that we were learning at the time. These included PCA and clustering alogrithms. These unsupervised learning techniques were used to reduce the number of features we were working with to try to make the model more generalized instead of overfitting it to the data we had. Child Seating Algorithm

Back

In around August of 2022, the DOT formally announced that it would be requesting that airlines within the United States make a conscious effort to make sure that children were always sitting with an adult from their booking next to them on flights. While this sounds like common sense to most of us, traditionally this had not been the case for airlines. The majority of the airline industry uses one of only a few web-based products to handle all of their reservations and bookings and none of these innately had logic to seat passengers like this. Instead, most used some sort of randomized pattern based on how many people had checked in for their flight and the expected weight necessary to balance the plane for takeoff. Conversely, a family could choose seats, but for some carriers, this involved an extra cost. To meet with the DOT recommendation, I was asked to build an algorithm that would take children who had not had a seat purchased for them, and play Tetris with the bookings and open seats of the plane to both ensure that passengers under the age of 13 were sat with an adult from their booking, and that the plane was loaded in a way that risk of a tail tip was minimized. The code in my github is a generalized version of this algorithm that I am making available. I had asked if we as a company wanted to patent it, since only American and United had made the commitment to the DOT to seat children accordingly and both used a manual process to achieve this, however, no one seemed to be terribly concerned with the whole idea. So here I am making this open source in the hope that more airlines will use this to better the experience of their customers.
